History of Sri lankan Art

Sri Lankan arts have a rich and vibrant history that dates back thousands of years, reflecting the island nation's diverse cultural heritage. Rooted in ancient traditions and influenced by various civilizations, Sri Lankan arts have evolved into a unique fusion of indigenous, Buddhist, Hindu, and colonial influences. From exquisite temple paintings and intricate wood carvings to mesmerizing dance forms and melodious music, the artistic expressions of Sri Lanka have captivated audiences for centuries. The island's strategic location on ancient trade routes and its interactions with neighboring cultures have contributed to the dynamic development of its artistic traditions. This introduction will delve into the fascinating journey of Sri Lankan arts, exploring the key influences, artistic mediums, and significant milestones that have shaped this vibrant tapestry of creativity throughout history.



1st Century BCE

Buddha Statues


The Anuradhapura Period in Sri Lankan art, occurring in the 1st century BCE, was a significant milestone influenced by Indian Buddhist art. This period witnessed the creation of magnificent rock-cut Buddha statues and captivating cave temples. The statues, intricately carved into the rock faces, exuded serenity and spirituality, with meticulous details evoking a sense of transcendence. The cave temples, adorned with vibrant fresco paintings depicting Buddhist narratives, became important spiritual and cultural centers. Today, the legacy of these artistic marvels continues to captivate, offering a glimpse into Sri Lanka's ancient artistic heritage and its deep connection to Buddhism.

4th Century

Polannaruwa Achitecture

The Polonnaruwa Period, spanning from the 4th to the 10th century CE, was a time of flourishing art and architecture in Sri Lanka, supported by the patronage of Sinhalese kings. This era left behind notable artistic achievements that continue to captivate. The construction of grand stupas, or dagobas, stood as a testament to the architectural prowess of the time, with intricate designs and a profound sense of devotion. Exquisite stone sculptures, displaying intricate details and expressive features, showcased the remarkable craftsmanship of skilled artisans. Additionally, the development of vibrant frescoes, adorning temple walls and royal buildings, added a sense of beauty and grandeur to the artistic landscape. The Polonnaruwa Period remains a golden age for Sri Lankan art and architecture, with its grand stupas, intricate stone sculptures, and vibrant frescoes serving as lasting reminders of the cultural and artistic legacy of this period.

11th Century CE

Kandyan Jataka Tales

During the Kandyan Period, the Kandyan Kingdom rose to prominence, and a fusion of indigenous and South Indian artistic traditions took place. Notably, temple murals depicting Jataka tales, which are stories of the previous lives of Buddha, became prevalent. These murals showcased remarkable artistic skills, with vibrant colors and intricate details bringing the narratives to life. The Kandyan Period marked a significant era in Sri Lankan art, characterized by the blending of cultural influences and the creation of visually captivating temple murals that served as important religious and artistic expressions.

16th Century CE

European Art

the Colonial Period in Sri Lanka introduced new artistic elements and techniques through the influences of Portuguese, Dutch, and British colonial powers. European artistic styles left their mark on Sri Lankan art during this period. Notably, Christian art gained prominence, influenced by European religious art. Paintings, sculptures, and architecture reflected the fusion of European techniques and local traditions, resulting in unique artistic expressions that showcased the dynamic cultural exchange between Sri Lanka and the colonial powers.

1990

Contempary Era

In the post-independence period, Sri Lankan art underwent diversification as artists explored various mediums and themes. Traditional arts continued to thrive, representing the country's cultural heritage, while contemporary artists addressed social, political, and cultural issues through their works. Artists embraced a wide range of mediums, including painting, sculpture, ceramics, and installation art, reflecting the evolving nature of artistic expressions. The Contemporary Era in Sri Lankan art is marked by the freedom of expression and the ability of artists to engage with current societal issues, showcasing the dynamic and ever-evolving nature of the country's artistic landscape.
